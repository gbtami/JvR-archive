Moscow

1988

 

The 55th Soviet championship was played in Moscow from 25 July - 18 August 1988. All chess stars participated for the first time since 1973. It would become the last great Soviet championship.

Eighteen players took part. Beliavsky, Salov and Ehlvest qualified as the top three of the previous championship. Seven players were invited: world champion Kasparov, former world champions Karpov, Smyslov and Tal, and the candidates Yusupov and Sokolov.

Eight places were at stake in two preliminary rounds in 1987. Two Swiss tournaments were played at Norilsk and Pavlodar as qualification for two sections of the First League. Thereafter, Ivanchuk, Yudasin, Gavrikov and Gurevich qualified in Lvov. Furthermore, Smirin, Khalifman, Kharitonov and Malaniuk were the best in Sverdlovsk. Reserves became Eingorn, Chernin, Tseshkovsky, Gelfand and Novikov.

The Super League was played in Moscow’s new International Hotel. One-thousand seats were available for the audience in the playing hall. Mikhail Botvinnik functioned as senior arbiter. Tal drew against Vaganian in the first round. Yusupov was asked to play the second round in a hotel room due to Tal’s illness. Artur refused. The arbiters decided to replace Tal by Eingorn.

Why the 55th championship was so important, is not discussed in the sources, but I can reconstruct three factors:

1. The strongly increased prizes of the Super League made participation interesting for the stars.

2. A large group of young talents had entered the scene in recent years.

3. All players hoped to star and receive invitations by foreign tournaments. The acceptation had become easier, and Soviet players were allowed to keep half of the prize money. Especially, the GMA tournaments were financially attractive.

Many dull games were played in Moscow 1988. The ability to draw was the greatest progress in the Soviet championship: 36% of the games were drawn in Leningrad 1934, 47% in Moscow 1957 and 66% in Moscow 1988.

Kasparov and Karpov shared the first place with +6, =11. Beliavsky played the most interesting chess, shared the lead in the rounds 7 and 8, but ended with a level score. Salov fought hard in long games, despite his prophylactic style. Defeats against Kasparov and Yusupov made him descend to the fourth place. Yusupov lost against Karpov and Ivanchuk. A strong finish gained him the bronze medal. Eingorn as replacement and Ivanchuk as debutant did well. Yudasin became seventh, a fine result for someone who had to play two qualification tournaments in 1987. Smyslov started poorly but finished good.

High places in the tournament were taken by the collectors of advantages: Karpov, Yusupov and Salov. Kasparov claimed to be a dynamic player. Therefore, I searched for games with the finest characteristic of dynamism: giving material for time. The greatest example was played by ... Karpov against Yusupov. A careful collection of advantages in the endgame was made by ... Kasparov against Salov. The claim by Kasparov resulted from his arrogance. Adventurous times of Tal and Spassky were gone.

Normally a match of four games would have followed, because two players shared the first place. E.g. Beliavsky had defeated Salov with 3-1 shortly after the previous championship tournament. The two K’s started difficulties. A match was not played, despite the efforts of Botvinnik among others. Both players got a gold medal in December 1988. The following political developments were far more important. The Berlin wall fell in November 1989. A communist putsch against Gorbachov failed in 1991. When Jeltsin overtook power, the Soviet-Union ceased to exist. Therefore, the last Soviet chess championship was played in 1991.

Years before the collapse of the Soviet regime, cracks in the system became apparent. The world championship in 1997 was not played in the Soviet-Union but in Seville. The tournament book of Moscow 1988 was published in Russian and English as Chess in USSR, Soviet Tournament News Review 1. It was printed in Rome!

Soviet players got more liberty to participate in foreign tournaments. They were also allowed to keep a part of the prize money. When the restrictions ended in 1991, they flooded the Western tournaments. Twenty of the thirty-six best players of the world were former Soviet citizens. They dominated most super tournaments.
